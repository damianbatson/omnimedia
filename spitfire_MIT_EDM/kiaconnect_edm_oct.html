Kia is revving up its environmental credentials around the world in order to drive down the carbon footprint of it's vehicles and manufacturing processes.

And here in New Zealand we are doing our bit to make a difference, too.
Kia Motors NZ has embarked on a sustainability programme to gain CarboNZero Certification – which consists of a rigorous audit of our activities in New Zealand and how we can reduce and/or offset our carbon footprint, with the intention of becoming fully and transparently carbon neutral.

That puts Kia Motors NZ right up at the same level with its international parent, which is investing US$2.25 billion – 6% of total sales revenue – in research and development this year alone, much of it aimed at reducing the company's impact on the environment.

That level of investment has helped Kia to become 44% more eco-efficient than in 2004, when it began measuring its environmental impact. Some of that is due to improved manufacturing techniques, reduced use of hazardous chemicals/substances and greater recycling.

In fact, two of Kia's big manufacturing facilities in Korea produced no landfill waste whatsoever last year, helping to reduce its global production of waste materials by 28% compared to the 2003 level.

In Europe, Kia has been supporting the 'Planting for a Better Life' Mali project in West Africa, which has already planted 3.5 million trees since 2007. Kia promises to plant up to six million additional jatropha shrubs over the next three years, helping to secure a brighter future for communities in the area by boosting agricultural output and generating income for farmers and their families.

All those efforts are on top of the progress being made in the development of vehicles that use less fuel and emit fewer emissions, thanks to high-tech engines and transmissions now featuring in models like the Picanto, Rio, Cerato, Optima, Sportage and Sorento.

If that is not enough, Kia Motors has just announced that it will increase its research and development spend by a further 12% in 2013 to US$2.52 billion so that it can speed up the process.

Vehicles made by Kia are among the most efficient on the road, but with rising petrol and diesel prices it certainly pays to adopt good fuel-saving practices. Try these:

  • If your car's petrol, chances are it doesn't need high octane fuel. So save and fill up with 91.
  • Check the tyre pressures regularly. Under-inflated tyres create more road friction and increase fuel consumption.
  • If you're driving at motorway speed, open windows can create wind resistance and increase fuel use. If you want some fresh air, open all the air vents.
  • Don't leave the A/C on all the time. Using it occasionally could save you fuel. Carrying empty ski racks on your car roof creates wind drag. Fit them when needed.
  • Remove heavy items from your car boot if they are not required. More weight equals more fuel use.
  • Driving above the open road speed limit burns more fuel. Keep to the limit and save. it's safer, too.
  • And lastly, don't hold onto lower gears longer than you need to. Getting into higher gears early can make significant savings.
